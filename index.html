The Watergate Scandal

The Political Crisis That Changed America

What Was Watergate?

The Watergate scandal was a major political crisis in the United States during the early 1970s that ultimately led to the resignation of President Richard Nixon. It began with a break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate office complex in Washington, D.C., and evolved into a constitutional crisis involving abuse of power, obstruction of justice, and coverup at the highest levels of government.

The Break-In
June 17, 1972

On June 17, 1972, five men were arrested for breaking into the Democratic National Committee headquarters located in the Watergate complex. The burglars were caught attempting to wiretap phones and steal documents. Investigative journalism would soon reveal connections to President Nixon's reelection campaign.

The Smoking Gun Tape

The most damaging evidence against Nixon was a tape recording from June 23, 1972. It revealed Nixon discussing using the CIA to obstruct the FBI's investigation, proving his direct involvement in the coverup. This tape became known as the "smoking gun" and sealed his fate.

The Investigation and Coverup

Senior officials attempted to cover up involvement in the break-in. Nixon and aides engaged in obstruction of justice, abuse of power, and contempt of Congress. The Oval Office tapes became crucial evidence.

The Constitutional Crisis

Nixon refused to release tapes citing executive privilege. Supreme Court ruled he must release them in United States v. Nixon. House Judiciary Committee adopted articles of impeachment for obstruction, abuse of power, and contempt.

The Resignation

Facing certain impeachment, Nixon resigned on August 8, 1974. Gerald Ford became President and pardoned him one month later.

The Saturday Night Massacre

October 20, 1973: Nixon ordered firing of Special Prosecutor Cox. Attorney General Richardson resigned, Deputy AG Ruckelshaus fired, Solicitor General Bork executed order. Public outcry increased calls for impeachment.

Legacy
  • First and only resignation of a U.S. president
  • Led to major reforms in campaign finance laws
  • Strengthened the role of investigative journalism
  • 69 government officials were charged; 48 pleaded guilty
  • The suffix "-gate" became standard for political scandals
  • Demonstrated the importance of checks and balances in government
  • Resulted in the Ethics in Government Act of 1978
