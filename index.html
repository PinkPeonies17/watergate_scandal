The Watergate Scandal

The Political Crisis That Changed America Forever

What Was Watergate?

The Watergate scandal was a political crisis in the early 1970s that began with a break‑in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters and led to President Richard Nixon’s resignation. It revealed abuses of power, obstruction of justice, and cover‑up attempts at the highest levels of government.

The Break-In

On June 17, 1972, five men were arrested at the DNC headquarters in the Watergate complex while attempting to wiretap phones and steal documents. Initially reported as a burglary, investigative journalism revealed ties to Nixon's reelection campaign.

The Smoking Gun Tape

A tape recorded on June 23, 1972, revealed Nixon discussing using the CIA to obstruct the FBI investigation, confirming his involvement in the cover-up. This "smoking gun" led to his political downfall.

Timeline of Key Events

June 17, 1972 — Watergate break-in.

October 1972 — Investigation begins after media reporting.

January 1973 — Watergate burglars convicted.

April 30, 1973 — Haldeman and Ehrlichman resign.

July 1973 — White House taping system revealed.

October 20, 1973 — "Saturday Night Massacre."

July 24, 1974 — Supreme Court orders tapes released.

August 8, 1974 — Nixon resigns.

August 9, 1974 — Gerald Ford becomes President.

Richard Nixon

37th President of the U.S., resigned due to Watergate. Directly involved in cover-up.

Bob Woodward & Carl Bernstein

Journalists at The Washington Post whose reporting exposed the scandal and cover-up.

John Dean

White House Counsel who testified about Nixon’s involvement in the cover-up.

Investigation & Constitutional Impact

Congressional hearings, the Supreme Court ruling in United States v. Nixon, and media investigation revealed Nixon’s cover-up. Led to reforms in campaign finance and executive oversight.
