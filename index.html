Annotated Bibliography

FBI. “Watergate.” FBI.gov, Federal Bureau of Investigation, www.fbi.gov/history/famous-cases/watergate. Accessed 18 Jan. 2026.
This source is credible because it is published by the Federal Bureau of Investigation, the agency that investigated Watergate. It provides first-hand, factual information about the break-in, arrests, and legal proceedings, making it reliable for understanding the official law-enforcement perspective.

“Watergate Scandal.” Encyclopædia Britannica, Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc., www.britannica.com/event/Watergate-Scandal. Accessed 18 Jan. 2026.
This source is credible due to Britannica’s editorial oversight and reputation for accuracy. It offers a clear, organized historical summary of the scandal, including events, key figures, and political implications. Useful for background context and historical framing.

“Select Committee on Presidential Campaign Activities (Watergate Committee).” U.S. Senate, www.senate.gov/about/powers-procedures/investigations/watergate.htm. Accessed 18 Jan. 2026.
Credible because it is an official government source. It details the Senate’s investigative role, hearings, and constitutional considerations. Useful for understanding congressional oversight and legal procedures during Watergate.

“Watergate Scandal.” A&E Television Networks, www.history.com/articles/watergate. Accessed 18 Jan. 2026.
Credible due to professional historians and editors. Provides a narrative overview of the scandal, including break-in, cover-up, investigative journalism, and impact. Helpful for understanding the sequence of events and societal consequences.
